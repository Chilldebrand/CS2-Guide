# Ten essential principles for climbing

These are the habits with the highest return for a new player. Work on one or two at a time instead of trying to “play smarter” in every possible way.

## Essential Principle 1: Stop before you shoot

**Why it raises rank:** Moving accuracy makes easy kills miss.  
**Do:** Counter-strafe, then fire the first accurate burst.  
**Practice cue:** In a deathmatch, count only kills where your first bullet came after a full stop.  
**Failure mode:** Holding movement while spraying and blaming aim.

## Essential Principle 2: Keep the crosshair where the enemy can appear

**Why it raises rank:** You shorten the time between seeing an enemy and firing.  
**Do:** Pre-aim head height and the next likely angle, not the wall or floor.  
**Practice cue:** Watch one demo with your crosshair visible and mark three avoidable low placements.  
**Failure mode:** Looking at the floor while moving between angles.

## Essential Principle 3: Make every fight tradeable

**Why it raises rank:** A lost first duel can still become a favorable exchange.  
**Do:** Enter behind a teammate or keep a teammate close enough to swing on your death.  
**Practice cue:** Call “I’m second” or “trade me” before entering.  
**Failure mode:** Taking first contact alone and dying with no follow-up.

## Essential Principle 4: Use utility to change the fight

**Why it raises rank:** A smoke, flash, or molotov can remove an angle before aim is tested.  
**Do:** State the target and follow-up: “Flash top ramp, swing behind.”  
**Practice cue:** Buy at least one grenade that directly helps your next fight.  
**Failure mode:** Throwing grenades after the fight or using them because they are available.

## Essential Principle 5: Buy with the team

**Why it raises rank:** One synchronized round beats five isolated inventories.  
**Do:** Call full buy, force, half, or save before spending.  
**Practice cue:** Check teammates’ money and utility before purchasing.  
**Failure mode:** Buying an AWP or armor when the team is saving.

## Essential Principle 6: Communicate information, not emotion

**Why it raises rank:** Precise information lets teammates make decisions quickly.  
**Do:** Say location, number, damage, direction, and time: “Two B main, one tagged, moving out.”  
**Practice cue:** Replace blame with one actionable call after every death.  
**Failure mode:** “He’s lit” with no location or “why are you there?”

## Essential Principle 7: Fight for space, then keep it

**Why it raises rank:** Teams win more rounds by controlling useful areas than by chasing kills.  
**Do:** After taking space, hold the exit, watch the flank, and make the enemy spend utility.  
**Practice cue:** After every entry, ask “what space did that buy us?”  
**Failure mode:** Chasing a second kill into an untradeable angle.

## Essential Principle 8: Play the clock and the objective

**Why it raises rank:** Time pressure makes defenders rotate and attackers panic.  
**Do:** On T side, preserve enough time for the execute and plant; on CT side, make the attacker clear you.  
**Practice cue:** At 45 seconds, call whether the round needs a hit, split, or save.  
**Failure mode:** Saving utility until the round is already lost.

## Essential Principle 9: Review decisions, not just aim

**Why it raises rank:** A death is useful when it exposes a repeatable decision error.  
**Do:** After a match, find three deaths and label them as mechanics, spacing, information, timing, or economy.  
**Practice cue:** Choose one category for the next session.  
**Failure mode:** Only watching kills and concluding that you need more aim.

## Essential Principle 10: Be consistent before being clever

**Why it raises rank:** Reliable defaults, trades, and utility win more rounds than rare outplays.  
**Do:** Repeat a small playbook until opponents force an adjustment.  
**Practice cue:** Track whether your first three rounds used the same clear and trade rules.  
**Failure mode:** Changing role and route every round without learning from the result.
